1. Macroeconomic Context & Scenario Framework

On 28 February 2026, the US and Israel launched coordinated strikes on Iran (Operation Epic Fury), resulting in the death of Supreme Leader Khamenei and triggering Iranian retaliation across the Gulf. The IRGC declared the Strait of Hormuz closed on 2 March. An insurance-driven shutdown, rather than a conventional naval blockade, has reduced tanker transit by approximately 90%. Major shipping lines have suspended Hormuz operations and rerouted via the Cape of Good Hope.

The analysis that follows is structured around four scenarios for the period March–May 2026, synthesising published forecasts from Goldman Sachs, Westpac, CBA, Oxford Economics, UBS, and Allianz Research. These scenarios are descriptive and analytical; they do not imply any specific policy response.

2. Equity Markets

Global Equity Response

Global equities sold off in the first week of the conflict, with the S&P 500 falling 0.7% and the Dow dropping over 800 points by 5 March. European bourses fell 1–2%, reflecting their direct energy cost exposure. The VIX spiked as implied volatility repriced across asset classes.

ASX 200: Bifurcated Impact

The ASX 200 has experienced material sector rotation rather than a uniform drawdown. The index fell 1.3–1.4% in aggregate, but this masks extreme divergence between winners and losers. Energy producers (Woodside +30% YTD, Santos +25%) have surged, while consumer discretionary, transport, and travel stocks have been hit. This bifurcation creates portfolio-level risk for concentrated holdings and benchmarked funds.

3. Sovereign & Corporate Debt Markets

Government Bonds

Government bonds have defied the traditional safe-haven playbook. US Treasuries and Australian Government Bonds (AGBs) initially sold off as the oil surge reignited inflation expectations. The 10-year AGB yield rose as traders priced in a higher-for-longer RBA path.

This is a critical departure from prior geopolitical shocks: bonds are not providing portfolio insurance. The stagflationary nature of the shock (simultaneous upward pressure on inflation and downward pressure on growth) creates a poisonous dynamic for fixed income, as neither a flight-to-safety rally nor a growth-slowdown rally can materialise cleanly.

Corporate Debt & Issuance

The corporate bond market has seized. Bloomberg reported that new bond issuance in Europe was put on hold entirely in the first week of the conflict. The iTraxx Europe Crossover index (sub-investment-grade CDS) jumped ~11bps to 270bps.

In Australia, corporate issuance windows have narrowed. Spread widening is most pronounced in transport, logistics, and consumer-facing sectors. Energy-sector credits, conversely, have tightened as improved cash-flow outlooks reduce perceived default risk.

If the disruption persists (Scenarios B–D), corporates approaching refinancing windows face materially higher funding costs. This is especially significant for BBB-rated issuers near the investment-grade boundary, where a downgrade could trigger forced selling by mandated funds.

4. Derivatives & Commodities Markets

Oil Derivatives: Volatility Structure

The oil derivatives market is signalling that traders currently view the shock as short-lived. Brent 30-day at-the-money implied volatility jumped 17.5 points to 68%, but 60-day and 90-day tenors rose only 5.9 and 2.8 points respectively. This steep term structure inversion suggests the market is pricing a spike-and-revert pattern rather than a structural shift.

This market-implied view broadly supports Scenario A or an early resolution within Scenario B. However, if the situation persists and the term structure flattens or inverts further along the curve, it would signal that markets are repricing toward Scenarios C/D — with significant implications for hedging costs across the real economy.

Margin Call & Collateral Dynamics

The surge in energy prices and volatility has triggered large margin calls across commodity futures and OTC derivatives markets. VLCC tanker rates hitting an all-time high of $423,736/day (+94% in a single day) illustrates the magnitude of the repricing. Market participants with short energy exposure or long freight derivatives face significant margin pressure.

For Australian entities, the key transmission channels are:

  • ▲Energy hedging by corporates: Airlines (Qantas, Virgin), logistics firms, and large fuel consumers face mark-to-market losses on existing hedges or inability to roll hedges at viable prices.
  • ▲Bank trading books: Australian banks with commodity derivatives exposure face increased VaR and capital requirements. CCP margin requirements have escalated.
  • ▲Super fund derivative overlays: Funds using futures-based commodity exposure or currency hedges face margin calls requiring liquidity buffers.
  • ●Producer hedging windfall: Australian LNG and oil producers who sold forward are now deeply in-the-money, releasing collateral. Oil producers are locking in 2026 hedges at elevated prices.
Systemic risk note: In Scenarios C and D, the magnitude of margin calls could create a liquidity squeeze analogous to the March 2020 or September 2022 (UK LDI) episodes. CCP margin procyclicality — where rising volatility triggers rising margin requirements, forcing position liquidation that further increases volatility — is a well-documented amplification mechanism. ASIC and APRA's joint oversight of CCPs would need to monitor margin call cascades closely under these scenarios.

5. Credit Markets: Business & Consumer

Business Credit

Business Lending Conditions

APRA's most recent data shows credit growing at around long-term average rates, with arrears and NPLs remaining low despite recent increases. The Iran conflict introduces a two-track dynamic for business credit:

Sectors facing credit tightening

  • ▲Transport & logistics: Fuel cost surges directly compress margins. Smaller operators with thin capital buffers face cash-flow stress within weeks under Scenarios B–D.
  • ▲Hospitality & tourism: Consumer discretionary pullback + higher operating costs = margin compression. Already leveraged operators (post-COVID debt overhang) are most vulnerable.
  • ▲Agriculture: Fuel and fertiliser cost surges (approximately one-third of global fertiliser transits Hormuz) squeeze farm margins, particularly for those without hedging.
  • ▲Construction: Already stressed from higher rates; input cost escalation in energy-intensive materials compounds existing headwinds.

Sectors with improving credit profiles

  • ▼Energy producers: LNG, gas, coal, and oil producers see dramatically improved cash flows, debt-service coverage, and credit metrics.
  • ▼Mining services: Benefit from elevated commodity prices and increased exploration activity.
Consumer Credit

Household & Consumer Credit Conditions

Australian households enter this shock in a precarious position. Debt-to-income sits at 190%, mortgage stress already affects 24.5% of holders (1,187,000 people), and the RBA has just raised rates to 3.85%. The Iran conflict layers additional cost-of-living pressure on top of this existing strain.

Transmission to consumer credit quality

Rising fuel costs act as a regressive consumption tax that disproportionately affects lower-income and outer-suburban households — precisely the cohort already most stretched on mortgage repayments. Under Scenario B, petrol prices rise 30–50c/L, adding roughly $30–50/week to an average household's fuel bill. For a household already at the margin of mortgage serviceability, this is material.

6. Securitisation Markets

RMBS: The Central Vulnerability

Residential mortgage-backed securities constitute approximately three-quarters of funding for Australian non-bank mortgage lenders. The RBA's securitisation dataset, which covers over $300 billion of outstanding RMBS, provides the regulator with granular loan-level data to monitor emerging stress.

Current arrears on marketed RMBS are broadly in line with bank mortgage arrears — elevated compared to recent lows but not high by historical or international standards. The Iran conflict threatens this stability through two channels:

Channel 1: Underlying collateral deterioration

Rising mortgage stress (via fuel costs and potential rate rises) directly worsens the performance of the loan pools backing RMBS. Non-bank lenders, whose borrowers tend to have weaker credit profiles, are more exposed. Under Scenarios C/D, arrears could rise to levels that erode subordination in lower-rated tranches.

Channel 2: Spread widening and issuance disruption

Global risk-off sentiment and credit spread widening makes RMBS issuance more expensive. If primary issuance becomes unviable (Scenarios C/D), non-bank lenders face a funding squeeze — unable to originate new loans or refinance existing warehouse facilities. This can force portfolio sales at distressed prices or, in extreme cases, lender failure.

7. Insurance & Reinsurance

Marine & War-Risk Insurance: Ground Zero

The insurance market is not merely affected by the Hormuz crisis — it is the primary mechanism through which the closure has been effected. The withdrawal of war-risk cover by P&I clubs, rather than physical naval blockade, is what halted shipping. This has far-reaching consequences for the insurance industry itself.

  • ▲Rate escalation: Marsh estimates marine hull insurance rates in the Gulf may rise 25–50% absent direct attacks on merchant shipping, and 50–100%+ if attacks occur. War-risk premiums for Hormuz transit rose from 0.125% to 0.2–0.4% of vessel value — an increase of approximately $250,000 per transit for VLCCs.
  • ▲Reinsurance capacity withdrawal: Reinsurers (notably Skuld and others) are withdrawing capacity at short notice, citing aggregation risk from hundreds of ships clustered outside the strait. This tightening cascades through the reinsurance chain.
  • ▲April 1 renewal timing: The crisis coincides precisely with the 1 April reinsurance renewal — one of the year's most important pricing events. This timing means the conflict will directly influence treaty renewal terms, capacity allocations, and pricing across marine, energy, aviation, and property lines globally.

Australian General Insurance Impacts

For Australian general insurers (IAG, Suncorp, QBE), the primary transmission channels are:

  • ▲Reinsurance costs: Higher global reinsurance pricing flows through to Australian insurers at renewals. Even non-marine lines face repricing as reinsurers rebuild capital.
  • ▲Investment portfolios: Insurers hold significant fixed-income portfolios. Bond sell-offs create mark-to-market losses, compressing capital adequacy ratios.
  • ●Claims dynamics: No direct claims impact from the conflict for domestic lines, but business interruption claims may emerge if supply-chain disruptions extend.
  • ▲QBE global exposure: As Australia's largest global insurer, QBE's marine, energy, and aviation lines face direct claims and reserving pressure.

8. Superannuation

System-Level Exposure

Australia's $4 trillion+ superannuation system is the world's fourth-largest pool of retirement savings and the dominant institutional investor in Australian markets. The conflict affects super funds through multiple channels, with the significance depending heavily on which scenario materialises.

Listed equities (typically 40–50% of balanced funds)

The sector bifurcation in equities described in Section 2 flows directly into super fund returns. Funds with overweight positions in energy benefit; those overweight consumer discretionary, travel, or retail face underperformance. Index-tracking strategies capture the net effect. Active managers' relative performance will be significantly affected by their sector positioning entering the crisis.

Fixed income (typically 15–20%)

The failure of bonds to rally during this shock (see Section 3) means fixed-income allocations are not providing their expected diversification benefit. In a stagflationary scenario (C/D), both equities and bonds underperform simultaneously — the worst outcome for traditional balanced portfolios.

Unlisted assets (typically 20–30%: infrastructure, property, private equity, private credit)

These are discussed in detail in Section 9. The key issue for super is that unlisted asset valuations lag listed markets. If listed assets fall materially (Scenarios C/D), the stale pricing of unlisted assets creates a valuation gap that may incentivise member switching — a potential liquidity risk if redemption volumes exceed liquid asset buffers.

This was a key concern during COVID and would re-emerge in a severe scenario.

9. Private Markets: Credit & Equity

Private Credit: A Pre-Existing Condition Meets a New Shock

The private credit market enters the Iran crisis already under significant stress. In the first quarter of 2026, two landmark events have underscored structural vulnerabilities:

BlackRock / HPS Corporate Lending Fund

The $26 billion fund received ~$1.2 billion in withdrawal requests (9.3% of NAV) in Q1 2026. BlackRock imposed redemption gates — the first time it has limited withdrawals from a flagship private credit vehicle.

Blackstone BCRED

The $82 billion BCRED fund faced $3.7 billion in redemption requests in Q1 2026 ($1.7 billion in the latest period alone), echoing the BREIT episode of 2022–2023 but in the credit space.

The Iran conflict amplifies these existing vulnerabilities through several channels:

  • ▲Accelerated redemption pressure: Broader market volatility and risk-off sentiment are likely to increase redemption requests at private credit funds, precisely when the underlying loans are most difficult to liquidate.
  • ▲Underlying credit quality: Private credit portfolios are concentrated in mid-market corporate loans. Borrowers in transport, logistics, hospitality, and consumer-facing sectors face cash-flow compression from rising energy costs. Default risk is rising in these sub-sectors.
  • ▲Valuation opacity: Private credit assets are not marked-to-market in real time. NAVs may not reflect deteriorating credit quality for weeks or months, creating the conditions for a sudden repricing event.
  • ▲Contagion via super funds: Australian superannuation funds have significantly increased private credit allocations in recent years. ASIC's 2025–26 Corporate Plan confirmed surveillance of retail and wholesale private credit funds, focusing on governance, valuation, liquidity, and disclosure. The Iran conflict may accelerate the timeline on any regulatory findings.

Private Equity: Valuation & Exit Headwinds

Private equity faces a different but related set of challenges. APRA has maintained heightened supervision of unlisted asset valuations since August 2024, and the conflict introduces fresh headwinds:

  • ▲Exit market closure: IPO windows, which were already narrow, effectively close under Scenarios B–D. PE managers who have been holding out for higher valuations face further delays in returning capital to investors.
  • ▲Denominator effect: If listed equity falls materially (Scenarios C/D) while PE portfolios are marked slowly, PE allocations rise as a percentage of total portfolio — the "denominator effect." This can trigger automatic rebalancing sales, forced at distressed prices into illiquid secondary markets.
  • ▲Portfolio company stress: PE-owned companies typically carry higher leverage than publicly-listed peers. Rising interest rates (RBA tightening) + rising input costs (fuel, energy) = double compression on cash flows and debt-service coverage.
  • ▲Fundraising chill: LP appetite for new PE commitments declines during periods of market stress and uncertainty, constraining the primary fundraising market.

10. Systemic Risk & Cross-Sector Interactions

Reinforcing Feedback Loops

The most significant financial stability concern is not any single market's response, but the interaction effects between markets under Scenarios C and D. Several reinforcing feedback loops are possible:

Loop 1: RBA – Consumer Credit – Securitisation

Energy-driven inflation forces the RBA to maintain or raise rates. Higher rates increase mortgage stress. Mortgage arrears rise, worsening RMBS collateral performance. RMBS spreads widen, raising funding costs for non-bank lenders. Non-bank lenders tighten credit or face failure, further constraining household credit availability.

Loop 2: Private Markets – Superannuation – Liquidity

Listed markets fall while private market valuations lag. The valuation gap incentivises member switching. Switching volumes drain liquid assets. Funds may be forced to sell liquid holdings at distressed prices or gate redemptions. Fire sales further depress listed markets.

Loop 3: Derivatives – Margin – Liquidity

Rising energy prices and volatility increase margin requirements. Margin calls drain cash from market participants. Forced position liquidation increases volatility. Higher volatility triggers further margin increases. This CCP procyclicality loop is a well-known systemic amplifier.

Loop 4: Insurance – Trade Finance – Real Economy

Withdrawal of war-risk insurance halts shipping. Shipping disruption starves industry of raw materials and fuel. Business cash flows deteriorate. Business loan arrears rise. Banks tighten lending standards. Credit contraction deepens the economic downturn.

The interaction between energy-driven inflation, RBA policy responses, and household leverage is the single most consequential transmission channel for Australia.
