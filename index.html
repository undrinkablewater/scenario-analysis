1. Situation Overview

On 28 February 2026, the United States and Israel launched coordinated airstrikes on Iran under Operation Epic Fury, targeting military facilities, nuclear sites, and leadership. Iran's Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei was killed. Iran retaliated with missile and drone attacks on US bases and Israeli-linked targets in Gulf states, and the IRGC declared the Strait of Hormuz closed to all vessel traffic on 2 March 2026.

The closure has been achieved primarily through an insurance-driven shutdown rather than a conventional naval blockade. Insurers withdrew war-risk coverage, making it economically impossible for commercial vessels to transit, and major shipping lines (Maersk, MSC, Hapag-Lloyd, CMA CGM) suspended all Hormuz operations and began rerouting around the Cape of Good Hope.

IMF Transmission Rule of Thumb

4. Australia-Specific Impact Analysis

Australia's unique position: The economy faces a complex mix of headwinds (fuel import dependence, inflation passthrough) and tailwinds (energy export revenues from LNG, coal, and gas). The net effect depends heavily on the duration of the disruption and the price trajectory of key commodities.
⚠

Fuel Import Vulnerability

Australia imports approximately 90% of its refined liquid fuels, primarily from Singapore-based refineries that in turn source over 80% of their crude from the Middle East. Current domestic reserves sit at just 36 days of petrol, 34 days of diesel, and 32 days of jet fuel—well below the IEA's 90-day benchmark.

Headwinds

  • ▲Consumer fuel costs: NRMA expects pump prices up ~10% immediately; SBS projects up to $1/L more under a 3-month closure.
  • ▲Inflation passthrough: Transport costs feed into food, logistics, and services prices with a 1–3 month lag.
  • ▲Interest rate pressure: RBA Governor Bullock warned the board is "very alert" to inflation risks; a rate hike becomes likely if oil stays above $100.
  • ▲Reserve inadequacy: 36 days of petrol reserves vs. 90-day IEA benchmark; Australia is the worst-positioned of 27 IEA net oil importers.
  • ▲Indirect supply chain: Singapore refineries depend on Middle East crude; disruption cascades even without direct Hormuz exposure.

Tailwinds

  • ▼LNG export revenue: Australian LNG producers (Woodside, Santos) see windfall gains. Woodside up ~30% YTD. Santos Barossa project well-timed.
  • ▼Coal price surge: UBS sees thermal coal at US$130–140/t as Europe switches from gas to coal, benefiting Australian exporters.
  • ▼Terms of trade boost: Higher energy export prices partially offset import cost increases, supporting the current account.
  • ▼AUD resilience: Barclays notes Australia's commodity-linked currency has held up relatively well, dampening traditional risk-off correlations.
  • ▼Domestic production: Oil refineries reportedly supplied through May; some buffer exists.

5. RBA & Financial Markets Implications

The RBA meets next on 17 March 2026, with the cash rate currently at 3.85% following a February hike. The Iran conflict introduces a classic stagflationary dilemma: energy-driven inflation pushes rates up, while growth uncertainty argues for a hold.

ASX & Currency Dynamics

The ASX 200 has been relatively stable, reflecting offsetting forces: energy stocks surging (Woodside +30% YTD) while consumer discretionary and transport sectors face headwinds. The AUD initially fell to the mid-0.70s but has shown resilience at ~0.71, supported by rising commodity export revenues.

Under prolonged disruption scenarios, the AUD would weaken as risk-off sentiment overwhelms the commodity support. A move below 0.68 (Scenario C) or 0.64 (Scenario D) would itself amplify imported inflation, creating a negative feedback loop for the RBA.

6. Policy Considerations

Near-Term (March–April 2026)

  • ●Fuel reserve management: Consider accelerated drawdown plans if disruption extends beyond 3 weeks; coordinate with IEA on collective stockpile release.
  • ●Consumer price mitigation: Evaluate temporary excise relief on fuel if pump prices exceed $2.50/L, balanced against fiscal cost.
  • ●Supply diversification: Accelerate negotiations with non-Hormuz crude suppliers; support refinery feedstock switching.
  • ●Communication: Prevent panic-buying through transparent reserve reporting (government has already advised against panic-buying).

Medium-Term (May 2026 and Beyond)

  • ●Strategic reserves: Accelerate progress toward IEA 90-day compliance; current 34–36 day position is dangerously exposed.
  • ●Energy transition: The crisis reinforces the strategic case for electrification and renewable energy as a national security investment.
  • ●Export revenue management: LNG and coal windfall revenues could fund reserve-building and transition investment.
  • ●Refining capacity: Review domestic refining strategy; current reliance on Singapore processing amplifies Hormuz vulnerability.
