1. Macroeconomic Context & Scenario Framework

On 28 February 2026, the US and Israel launched coordinated strikes on Iran (Operation Epic Fury), resulting in the death of Supreme Leader Khamenei and triggering Iranian retaliation across the Gulf. The IRGC declared the Strait of Hormuz closed on 2 March. An insurance-driven shutdown, rather than a conventional naval blockade, reduced tanker transit by approximately 90%. Major shipping lines suspended Hormuz operations and rerouted via the Cape of Good Hope.

Seven weeks later, the situation has evolved through a turbulent and unpredictable trajectory. What follows is first a timeline of key developments since the original briefing, then an updated scenario framework for the period ahead.

Conflict Timeline: 8 March – 22 April 2026

11 March: UN Security Council passed resolution demanding end of attacks on shipping and reaffirming the right of vessels to traverse the Strait of Hormuz.
Late March: Iran launched hundreds of drones and ballistic missiles at Israel and US bases in Gulf states (Bahrain, Jordan, Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, UAE). March saw one of the largest monthly oil price jumps on record: Brent +51%.
1 April: Australian government halved fuel excise — 50% cut (26.3c/L) for 3 months until 30 June, plus additional 10.9% via state deal. Total reduction: 32c/L. Cost: $2.55 billion.
5 April: Trump threatened to attack Iran’s power plants and bridges if Hormuz not reopened.
6 April: Indirect talks in Islamabad led to 45-day ceasefire proposal. Iran declined.
7–8 April: Trump announced 2-week ceasefire on Truth Social. Iran agreed. Iran to reopen Strait of Hormuz.
9–16 April: Ships still not transiting Hormuz despite ceasefire. Insurance and security concerns remained.
17 April: Iran FM Araghchi declared Hormuz open to all shipping. Brent dropped 11%, fell below $91. ~20 ships transited on Saturday. RBA held cash rate at 4.10%. But confusion: ships required to coordinate with Iranian forces; ships with cargo linked to “hostile nations” not permitted.
18 April: Iran reversed course, re-closed Hormuz, stating US blockade of Iranian ports violated ceasefire. Traffic halted after attacks on commercial vessels.
20 April: Oil prices spiked again. Brent back above $95 on Hormuz standoff.
21 April: Trump extended ceasefire, said it would remain in place until Iran submits “unified proposal” to end conflict.
22 April: Iran seized two ships (MSC Francesca and Epaminondas) in Hormuz. Iran’s parliament declared it would not reopen Hormuz while US blockade remains. Brent at $101.73 (+3.3%). Trump gave Iran 3–5 days to engage or resume attacks.

What Actually Happened vs. Original Scenarios

Reality has tracked an oscillating B/C path. The Strait has been effectively closed for nearly 8 weeks. A ceasefire was agreed but has partially collapsed. Oil hit $120 at peak and is now ~$101. There is no Scenario D-level multi-theatre expansion, but Scenario A (swift resolution) clearly did not materialise.

The situation is best described as periods of hope (ceasefire announcement, brief reopening on 17 April) followed by reversals (re-closure on 18 April, ship seizures on 22 April). This “sawtooth” pattern creates its own form of market stress — uncertainty about the direction of travel is arguably more damaging to investment and risk management than a clear, sustained escalation.

2. Equity Markets

Updated with actual data

ASX 200: From Drawdown to Tentative Recovery

The ASX 200 experienced a peak drawdown of 940 points (10.2%), falling to a low of 8,262 on 23 March as the full implications of the Hormuz closure became apparent. This substantially exceeded the original Scenario B estimate of −3% to −6%.

Since late March, the index has rallied back to the 8,900–9,000 consolidation range by mid-April, supported by the ceasefire announcement, the government’s fuel excise cut, and the RBA’s decision to hold in April. The recovery has been led by defensive sectors (healthcare, consumer staples) rather than energy, which has retreated ~8% from its early April peak as investors rotated away.

Energy stocks (Woodside, Santos) surged dramatically in the initial weeks of the crisis but have since partially unwound gains. The sector bifurcation that was merely emerging in the original briefing has played out as forecast, then begun to partially reverse as commodity prices pulled back from their peaks.

3. Sovereign & Corporate Debt Markets

Updated with actual data

Government Bonds

Government bonds have continued to defy the traditional safe-haven playbook, as forecast in the original briefing. The US 10-year Treasury yield has risen 5bp to 4.368%, reflecting persistent inflation expectations rather than a flight to safety.

The stagflationary nature of the shock — simultaneous upward pressure on inflation and downward pressure on growth — has continued to create a poisonous dynamic for fixed income. With the RBA at 4.10% and CBA forecasting headline CPI at 5.4% by mid-2026, the bond market faces the same dilemma identified in the original briefing but with real data now confirming the mechanism.

Corporate Credit & Spreads

Investment-grade credit spreads have widened from 0.83 percentage points and continue to trend higher. Morningstar noted the widening “could continue if oil prices stay elevated,” and under a prolonged scenario, IG spreads could reach 1.2pp.

High-yield spreads have moved to 3.46 percentage points and could widen to 4.5–5.0pp under a sustained disruption. Global bond issuance has been materially disrupted.

The April 1 reinsurance renewal and the ceasefire volatility have added a new dimension: investors are struggling to price risk for assets where the underlying geopolitical trajectory oscillates week-to-week. This uncertainty premium may persist even under Scenario A, as markets learn to price “ceasefire fragility.”

4. Derivatives & Commodities Markets

Updated with actual data

Oil Market: The Defining Commodity Story

The IEA has characterised this as “the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market,” with demand destruction estimated at 4–5 million barrels per day (approximately 5% of global supply). Oil’s trajectory has been extraordinary:

  • ▲Pre-conflict: Brent at ~$73/bbl
  • ▲Peak: Nearly $120/bbl — a 55% surge from pre-conflict levels
  • ▲March: +51% — one of the largest monthly oil price jumps on record
  • ▼17 April: Brent dropped 11% to below $91 on Hormuz reopening announcement
  • ▲20–22 April: Back above $95, then $101.73 on 22 April (+3.3%) after re-closure and ship seizures

The volatility pattern has shifted from the original briefing. The steep term structure inversion observed at 8 March (suggesting a spike-and-revert pattern) has flattened, indicating that markets are now pricing a more persistent disruption. This is consistent with the shift from Scenario A expectations toward B/C.

Margin Call & Collateral Dynamics (Updated)

The sustained elevation in energy prices has produced cumulative margin call pressures far exceeding initial estimates. The 17–18 April whipsaw (11% drop followed by immediate reversal) was particularly damaging — participants on both sides of the trade were caught, creating margin pressure in both directions within 48 hours.

  • ▲Energy hedging by corporates: Airlines and logistics firms face multi-billion-dollar mark-to-market losses on existing hedge books. Some have been forced to reduce hedge ratios, leaving them more exposed to further price swings.
  • ▲Bank trading books: VaR breaches and capital requirement escalations have been reported across multiple global banks.
  • ▲Gold as safe haven: Gold has surged to US$4,712/oz, confirming its traditional safe-haven role. However, the 2.2% single-day drop on 22 April suggests even gold is subject to liquidity-driven selling.
  • ●AUD/USD: Trading in the 0.70–0.71 range. The Australian dollar has been buffered by elevated commodity export prices (LNG, iron ore) offsetting risk-off pressure.
Systemic risk note (updated): The whipsaw dynamics of the ceasefire — sharp moves in both directions — are proving more operationally disruptive than a sustained move in one direction. CCP margin models calibrated for directional moves are less effective at handling this “sawtooth” pattern. The risk of a margin-call cascade remains elevated and may actually be higher under Scenario B (grinding stalemate with periodic false hope) than under a clean Scenario C escalation.

5. Credit Markets: Business & Consumer

Business Credit Updated

Business Lending Conditions

The two-track dynamic forecast in the original briefing has materialised as expected. The RBA has now hiked twice (to 4.10%), and the fuel cost surge has compressed margins in exposed sectors. Deloitte has cut GDP growth forecasts from 2.4% to 1.9% for 2026–27.

Sectors facing credit tightening

  • ▲Transport & logistics: Despite the fuel excise cut, net fuel costs remain significantly elevated. Smaller operators are experiencing cash-flow stress. The excise cut (32c/L total reduction) offsets roughly half the oil price increase at the pump.
  • ▲Hospitality & tourism: Consumer discretionary pullback + higher operating costs = margin compression. Stagflation fears are dampening consumer confidence.
  • ▲Agriculture: Fuel and fertiliser cost surges (approximately one-third of global fertiliser transits Hormuz) continue to squeeze farm margins.
  • ▲Construction: Already stressed from higher rates; input cost escalation in energy-intensive materials compounds existing headwinds. The rate at 4.10% is further tightening conditions.

Sectors with improving credit profiles

  • ▼Energy producers: LNG, gas, coal, and oil producers continue to see dramatically improved cash flows, with Brent at $101+ vs. ~$73 pre-conflict.
  • ▼Mining services: Benefit from elevated commodity prices. Gold miners in particular benefit from $4,712/oz gold.
Consumer Credit Updated

Household & Consumer Credit Conditions

The feared RBA-fuel cost interaction has materialised. The RBA has raised rates from 3.85% to 4.10%, and before the excise cut on 1 April, average unleaded was above $2.30/L nationally. The excise cut provided temporary relief — pump prices fell 30+ cents per litre — but the underlying oil price remains elevated.

Key data points since the original briefing:

  • ▲RBA rate path: Feb: 3.85% (+25bp), Mar: 4.10% (+25bp, narrowly voted), Apr: held at 4.10%
  • ▲Inflation expectations: Hit 6.9% in Australia — well above the RBA’s 2–3% target band
  • ▲Inflation forecasts: CBA: headline CPI to 5.4% by mid-2026, trimmed mean to peak at 3.8%. Deloitte: CPI peak at 4.9% in June 2026.
  • ▲Stagflation risk: RBA Deputy Governor Hauser warned of stagflation “nightmare” — an extraordinary public statement from a senior central banker.

6. Securitisation Markets

Updated with actual data

RMBS: Better Than Feared

The securitisation market has, to date, performed significantly better than the downside scenarios contemplated in the original briefing. Key developments:

  • ▼Arrears declining: Non-bank housing arrears remain slightly below 1% (close to bank levels). Remarkably, the share of loans 3+ months in arrears has actually declined over the past year to near pre-pandemic levels.
  • ▼Issuance resilient: Firstmac priced a $2.4 billion RMBS deal in March — the largest in its history. Over 70% was bought by local investors, demonstrating continued domestic appetite for Australian RMBS.
  • ●RBA assessment: The March 2026 Financial Stability Review found that risks from non-bank lenders are “limited” by their relatively small size.
  • ▲Growth differential: Non-bank credit is growing faster than bank credit, a trend that warrants continued monitoring even though current arrears are benign.

This is broadly consistent with an early Scenario B outcome — stress is present but manageable, and the Australian RMBS market’s structural features (high subordination levels, strong servicer standards, RBA oversight) are functioning as intended.

7. Insurance & Reinsurance

Updated with actual data

Marine & War-Risk Insurance: Extreme Dislocation

The insurance dynamics that were identified as central to the Hormuz closure mechanism in the original briefing have intensified dramatically. Key developments since 8 March:

  • ▲War risk premium surge: Premiums surged fivefold within 48 hours of the conflict’s escalation. This far exceeded the original estimate of 25–50% increases.
  • ▲Replacement coverage: Where available, replacement coverage is now offered at roughly 60 times pre-crisis rates. For a VLCC, this represents an effective cost that makes many shipping routes commercially unviable.
  • ▲April 1 renewal outcome: The reinsurance renewal saw dislocation concentrated in specialty lines (marine war risk, energy, political violence). Property-catastrophe renewal was not directly affected, providing some sectoral insulation.
  • ●Government as insurer of last resort: The US Development Finance Corporation (DFC) announced a $40 billion reinsurance facility for Gulf shipping — an extraordinary intervention. The WEF has described governments as becoming “insurers of last resort.”

Australian General Insurance Impacts (Updated)

For Australian general insurers (IAG, Suncorp, QBE), the transmission channels identified in the original briefing are playing out:

  • ▲Reinsurance costs: April 1 renewals confirmed higher pricing in specialty lines. Marine and energy lines most affected. Property-cat renewals were a relative bright spot.
  • ▲Investment portfolios: The stagflationary environment continues to create mark-to-market losses on insurer fixed-income portfolios.
  • ●$40B DFC facility: The US government backstop for Gulf shipping insurance partially alleviates the total withdrawal of private capacity but raises questions about moral hazard and the sustainability of government-backed coverage.
  • ▲QBE global exposure: QBE’s marine, energy, and aviation lines face direct reserving pressure from the 60x premium surge and concentrated Gulf exposure.

9. Private Markets: Credit & Equity

Critical Update

Private Credit: Industry-Wide Stress Now Evident

The original briefing identified BlackRock/HPS and Blackstone BCRED as harbingers of broader private credit stress. Seven weeks later, the data confirms this was the leading edge of an industry-wide phenomenon. The private credit boom is “starting to crack.”

Updated Fund-Level Data

BlackRock / HPS Corporate Lending Fund

Imposed a 5% redemption cap. Received $1.57 billion in Q1 redemption requests but could only honour approximately one-third. This is more severe than the $1.2B reported in the original briefing.

Blackstone BCRED

Faced $3.7 billion in Q1 redemptions. Blackstone took the extraordinary step of raising its redemption limit to 7% and injecting $400 million alongside employees to stabilise the fund.

Industry-Wide Redemption Data: Q1 2026

The following chart shows Q1 2026 redemption requests as a percentage of fund NAV across the four largest private credit platforms. These numbers are unprecedented and confirm that the stress is systemic rather than idiosyncratic:

Blue Owl at 21.9%: Nearly one-quarter of Blue Owl’s private credit fund NAV was subject to redemption requests in a single quarter. This level of redemption pressure, if sustained, would require either massive gating, asset fire sales, or external capital injection. Blue Owl’s number alone represents a potential systemic event for the private credit sector.

The Iran conflict amplifies these pre-existing vulnerabilities through the channels identified in the original briefing, but with greater force:

  • ▲Accelerated redemption pressure: The broader risk-off environment and stagflation fears are driving institutional investors to reduce illiquid exposures. The ceasefire whipsaw (hope then disappointment) may be particularly damaging for investor confidence.
  • ▲Underlying credit quality: With Brent at $101+ and the RBA at 4.10%, borrowers in transport, logistics, hospitality, and consumer-facing sectors face double compression: rising input costs AND rising debt-service costs. Default risk is materially higher than at 8 March.
  • ▲Valuation opacity: NAVs still likely do not fully reflect deteriorating credit quality. The gap between reported NAVs and fair value is probably widening.
  • ▲Contagion via super funds: Australian superannuation funds’ private credit allocations are exposed to these same dynamics. ASIC’s surveillance of private credit funds takes on added urgency.

Private Equity: Valuation & Exit Headwinds (Updated)

Private equity continues to face the headwinds identified in the original briefing, now compounded by seven additional weeks of elevated uncertainty:

  • ▲Exit market: IPO windows remain effectively closed. The ASX 200’s recovery to 8,900–9,000 has not been sufficient to reopen the IPO pipeline, as uncertainty about the conflict’s trajectory deters both issuers and investors.
  • ▲Denominator effect: The March drawdown created a brief denominator effect that partially reversed in April. Under Scenarios C/D, this would return with greater force.
  • ▲Portfolio company stress: PE-owned companies are now facing the compounded effect of two RBA hikes (to 4.10%) plus seven weeks of elevated energy costs. Cash-flow and debt-service coverage ratios are deteriorating in exposed sectors.
  • ▲Fundraising: LP appetite for new commitments has further declined as institutional investors focus on liquidity management and de-risking.

10. Systemic Risk & Cross-Sector Interactions

Reinforcing Feedback Loops (Updated Assessment)

The four feedback loops identified in the original briefing remain the key systemic risk channels. Seven weeks of actual data allows a more informed assessment of which loops are activating and which remain latent:

Loop 1: RBA – Consumer Credit – Securitisation PARTIALLY ACTIVATED

This loop has partially activated. The RBA raised rates twice (to 4.10%), increasing mortgage stress. However, the fuel excise cut has dampened the consumer impact, and RMBS arrears have actually improved. Current severity: 4/10. The 1 July excise cliff could push this to 6–7/10 if oil remains above $100.

Loop 2: Private Markets – Superannuation – Liquidity PARTIALLY ACTIVATED

Private credit redemption data (8–22% in Q1) confirms this loop is activating at the private credit level. However, super fund member switching has remained normal, breaking the amplification chain. Current severity: 5/10. This would jump to 8/10 if a major private credit fund fails or suspends entirely.

Loop 3: Derivatives – Margin – Liquidity LATENT

Despite extreme oil volatility, no major margin-call cascade has materialised. The VIX at 18.92 suggests derivative market stress is being managed. The 17–18 April whipsaw was the highest-risk moment. Current severity: 3/10. A clean break above $120 oil would rapidly escalate this to 7–8/10.

Loop 4: Insurance – Trade Finance – Real Economy ACTIVATED

This is the most clearly activated loop. Insurance withdrawal continues to be the primary mechanism blocking Hormuz shipping. The $40B DFC facility is an attempt to break this loop, but its effectiveness is uncertain. The 60x premium surge and the re-closure on 18 April demonstrate the fragility of any insurance-led reopening. Current severity: 7/10.
